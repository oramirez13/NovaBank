What is Phishing?

How cybercriminals trick you into giving away your personal information and how to protect yourself.

What is Phishing?

Phishing is a type of cyberattack where criminals impersonate trusted organizations — banks, tech companies, or government agencies — to trick victims into revealing sensitive information such as passwords, credit card numbers, or personal identification data.

The word "phishing" is a play on "fishing": attackers cast a wide net with mass emails or fake websites and wait for victims to take the bait.

How Does It Work?

A typical phishing attack follows these steps:

  1. 1. Crafting the bait: The attacker creates a fake email or website that looks identical to a legitimate one.
  2. 2. Sending the hook: The victim receives an urgent message — "Your account has been suspended!", "Unauthorized access detected!" — designed to cause panic and bypass critical thinking.
  3. 3. The trap: The victim clicks a link or downloads a file, leading them to a fake page or installing malware on their device.
  4. 4. Data theft: Credentials, financial data, or system access are captured by the attacker.
Did you notice? The page you just came from (index.html) is an example of steps 1 and 2 — a realistic-looking fake bank alert designed to create urgency.

Red Flags to Watch For

Train yourself to spot these warning signs in emails and websites:

Suspicious Sender

The email address does not match the official domain. Example: [email protected] instead of [email protected]

Artificial Urgency

Phrases like "Act now!", "Within 24 hours", or "Account suspended" are designed to make you panic and act without thinking.

Suspicious Links

Hover over links before clicking. The URL shown may differ from where it actually leads. Look for misspellings or unusual domains.

Unexpected Attachments

Legitimate banks and companies will never ask you to download a "security tool" or verify your account by running a file.

How to Protect Yourself

  • Never download files from unsolicited emails.
  • Verify the sender's email address carefully, character by character.
  • Go directly to the official website by typing the URL — never through email links.
  • Enable two-factor authentication (2FA) on all your accounts.
  • Contact your bank directly if you receive a suspicious security alert.
  • Keep your antivirus updated and run regular scans.
